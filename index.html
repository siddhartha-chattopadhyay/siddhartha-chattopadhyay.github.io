About

Hi there! I am Siddhartha, a postdoc researcher at the Department of Physics, University of Central Florida. I am a theoretical AMO physicist specializing in ab-initio modeling of atomic and molecular structures and collision processes. My current research explores time-resolved attosecond electron dynamics following single and double ionization in atoms and molecules.



The advent of attosecond pulses has revolutionized the study of electronic motion in real-time, giving rise to the field of attosecond science in the early 2000s. This transformative field was honored with the 2023 Nobel Prize in Physics, awarded to Pierre Agostini, Anne L’Huillier, and Ferenc Krausz. With advancements in X-ray free-electron lasers and table-top XUV sources, we now have unprecedented opportunities for manipulating and controlling ultrafast electron dynamics, heralding the era of attosecond science 2.0.



Despite these technological advancements, theoretical approaches have yet to fully catch up. My goal is to develop methods based on a fully ab-initio description with finite pulses to simulate observables in an attosecond pump-probe spectroscopic experiments. Specifically, I aim to use XUV/X-ray pulses in multi-photon ionization to elucidate the formation of multiple hole states and their interactions with the environment.



To address these challenges, I combine close-coupling-based ab-initio structure methods (e.g., multi-configuration Hartree-Fock, multi-configuration Dirac-Hartree-Fock) with scattering theory approaches. This allows me to tackle complex phenomena relevant for the coherent control of atomic and molecular states and to understand the interaction of extreme light with matter.

    Research Interests

    Time-resolved studies of multiphoton double ionization

    X-ray Free-Electron Lasers have revolutionized the time-resolved study of multiple vacancies in poly-electronic atoms and molecules, yet theoretical advancements have lagged. To address this, we developed the finite-pulse virtual-sequential model (FPVSM), which simulates two-photon double-ionization processes in poly-electronic systems using ab initio single-ionization amplitudes for the initial and intermediate states. Implemented for poly-electronic atoms, FPVSM has shown remarkable agreement with experimental results for atomic neon. For the first time, we demonstrated that in the TPDI process of atomic neon, spatially separated entangled electrons cause two-particle interference in the joint photoelectron distribution, a nonlocal phenomenon first proposed by Anton Zeilinger (Nobel Prize in Physics 2022) and collaborators. Currently, we are integrating FPVSM into ASTRA, a molecular ionization program from Dr. Luca Argenti's group at UCF. This will enable the study of double-hole state formation and two-center two-particle interference, crucial for coherent control of molecular states and understanding of the ultrafast buildup of quantum entanglement through interaction of extreme light with matter.

    Multi-configuration Dirac-Hartree-Fock Close-coupling approach to atomic ionization

    Theoretical attosecond physics has traditionally focused on simple two-electron systems like He and H2 in its full dimensionality or used single- or two-active electron approximations for poly-electronic systems. With advances in intense X-FELs and XUV sources, studying spin-orbit effects in attosecond dynamics is on the horizon, requiring consideration of relaxation, dynamical electron correlation, and relativistic effects, especially for heavy atoms. This necessitates a unified treatment of bound states and continuum dynamics in a relativistic framework, beyond current truncated perturbative approaches. Our goal is to develop a multi-reference Dirac-Hartree-Fock treatment with close-coupling formalism. We plan to interface the general relativistic atomic structure package, GRASP2018 with a close-coupling formalism based on the NewStock suite of codes, surpassing current methods where relativistic terms in many-body attosecond processes are only treated as truncated perturbative expansions. This will open the avenues for studying spectral phases and the quantum beating observed in attosecond transient absorption spectroscopy of heavy atoms, where spin-orbit dynamics plays a crucial role.

    B-spline based basis set approach for ultrastrong laser-matter interactions

    The Schwinger effect, predicted by Julian Schwinger in 1951, is a quantum phenomenon where a strong electric field can spontaneously create particle pairs, like electron-positron pairs. Creating such ultra-strong fields in extreme light facilities will soon enable investigations into relativistic and vacuum polarization effects in the time domain. This requires an ab-initio relativistic treatment beyond the no-pair approximation. Solving the Dirac-Coulomb problem with finite difference basis faces issues due to spurious eigenstates and non-singular two-point boundary conditions. B-spline based algorithms can elegantly address the single-center Dirac equation. My goal is to develop a B-spline based basis set approach to solve the Dirac-Coulomb problem with lowest order QED effects to understand the time-resolved dynamics of pair-creation which would provide unprecedented insights into non-perturbative QED processes and the behavior of vacuum under extreme conditions. While the technical challenges are significant, the rapid advancements in both high-intensity laser technology and attosecond pulse generation make this a promising area of research.
